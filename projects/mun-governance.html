Overview

What is MUN Governance?

Model United Nations (MUN) is more than just a debate competition. It's a simulation of real-world diplomacy and governance. Behind every successful MUN conference is a team of organizers who design the rules, create the committees, write the scenarios, and evaluate the delegates. This is governance work—and it's just as important as the performance on stage.

My Role

As PMUN 2025-26 Chairperson, I designed the entire governance framework for the conference. This included creating committee charters, writing evaluation rubrics, designing marking schemes, and building systems to ensure fair and consistent judging. I also served as a delegate at multiple MUNs, giving me insight into what works and what doesn't from both sides.

Framework

How I Designed It

Effective governance requires balancing multiple competing interests: fairness, clarity, flexibility, and engagement. Here's how I approached it:

Key Principles

Transparency: All rules, criteria, and scoring systems are clearly documented and communicated upfront. No surprises or hidden criteria.
Fairness: Rubrics are designed to be objective and verifiable. Multiple judges can score the same delegate and get similar results.
Inclusivity: Rules accommodate different delegation sizes, experience levels, and backgrounds. No one is left behind.
Flexibility: Systems are designed to handle unexpected situations (missing delegates, technical issues, etc.) without falling apart.
Engagement: Rules and scenarios are designed to encourage participation and reward creativity, not just memorization.
Committee Design

Crafting Scenarios

A good MUN committee isn't just about the topic—it's about the scenario. The scenario sets the stage, defines the stakes, and creates opportunities for delegates to shine. Here's my approach:

Scenario Design Process

Choose a Real Issue: Start with a genuine global problem (climate change, conflict, inequality, etc.). Delegates should care about the topic.
Create Stakes: Design a scenario where decisions have real consequences. Delegates should feel the weight of their choices.
Define Positions: Each delegate gets a country or organization with a specific position. These should create natural tensions and alliances.
Build in Complexity: Add hidden agendas, conflicting interests, and moral dilemmas. Real diplomacy is messy.
Plan Crisis Moments: Introduce unexpected events that force delegates to adapt and respond creatively.

Example: Charter of the Reclaimed World

This was a post-apocalyptic crisis committee. The scenario: Civilization has collapsed. Delegates represent different survivor groups trying to rebuild society. The challenge: How do you create a fair, functional government from scratch when resources are scarce and trust is broken?

Stakes
Survival. Every decision affects resource allocation and group survival.
Complexity
Different groups have different priorities (farmers want land, scientists want research facilities, military wants security).
Creativity
Delegates had to invent new governance structures, not just apply existing models.
Crisis
Midway through, a natural disaster forces delegates to adapt their plans and respond in real-time.
Evaluation

Judging & Scoring

Fair evaluation is the backbone of a good MUN. I designed a multi-dimensional rubric that assesses delegates across several key areas:

Evaluation Dimensions

Research (20 points): How well does the delegate understand their country's position, the committee topic, and the broader context? Are facts accurate and relevant?
Diplomacy (25 points): Can the delegate build alliances, negotiate compromises, and work with others? Do they listen and adapt?
Public Speaking (20 points): Is the delegate clear, confident, and persuasive? Do they make eye contact and engage the audience?
Crisis Response (20 points): How does the delegate handle unexpected situations? Do they stay in character and think creatively?
Committee Participation (15 points): Is the delegate engaged throughout? Do they contribute meaningfully to discussions?

Why This Works

This rubric rewards well-rounded delegates, not just the loudest speakers. A delegate can win by being a brilliant negotiator, even if they're not the best public speaker. It also encourages judges to look for different types of excellence, not just one narrow definition of success.

Key Insights from Competing

Clarity Matters: Delegates need to understand the rules clearly. Ambiguity causes frustration and unfair outcomes.
Flexibility Wins: The best delegates adapt to unexpected situations. Governance should reward this.
Fairness Builds Trust: When delegates trust that judging is fair, they compete harder and have more fun.
Scenarios Matter Most: A brilliant scenario can make an average committee great. A boring scenario can kill even a well-run conference.
